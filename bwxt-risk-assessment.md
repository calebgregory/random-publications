# BWXT HPDU Facility Expansion — Risk Assessment & Findings

## Background

BWX Technologies (BWXT) has a $1.6 billion DOE/NNSA contract to become the nation's sole producer of high-purity depleted uranium (HPDU) metal, used for nuclear weapons maintenance and tank armor. They are expanding their existing Jonesborough, TN facility — an industrial site for 55+ years — and rezoned ~56 acres of agricultural land (A-1) to heavy industrial (M-2).

- **Planning Commission** voted 4-2 to recommend denial of the rezoning
- **County Commission** approved it 10-5 on 2026-03-23
- BWXT stated that if rezoning were denied, they'd build on their existing M-2 parcel with less buffer distance from neighbors

**The process:** DU oxide feedstock (U3O8) from Paducah, KY or Portsmouth, OH is chemically converted through multiple steps — dissolution in nitric acid, purification, fluorination with anhydrous hydrogen fluoride (HF), metal reduction — to produce HPDU metal. Pilot production (~20 MT/year) starts in existing buildings by end of 2026, scaling to full production (300-400 MT/year) in purpose-built facilities by ~2030.

**My context:** I live ~5 miles west (downwind) of the facility. The rezoning is approved. My goal is to rationally understand the risk, monitor the regulatory process going forward, and advise friends in the community.

## Key Risk Dimensions

### 1. Air Emissions — Routine Operations

- BWXT's own permit filing to TDEC used worst-case bounding assumptions (80% filtration efficiency instead of the >99% they expect) and showed emissions below TDEC's "insignificant activity" threshold — less than 0.1 mrem/year radiological dose, less than 5 tons/year per contaminant
- TDEC independently classified the pilot plant as an insignificant activity on 6/9/2025 — no air quality permit required
- Community critics cite ~250 lbs/year of uranium dust from permit filings; BWXT says actual emissions will be "grams per year" and the permit numbers are intentionally conservative bounding cases
- The permitted HF emissions figure is 474.5 lbs/year
- No project-specific air dispersion modeling has been done yet — that comes during NEPA review

**At 5 miles downwind:** Even under pessimistic assumptions, routine radiological dose is vanishingly small relative to the ~300 mrem/year natural background. HF is acutely toxic and denser than air but dissipates well before 5 miles under normal atmospheric conditions. Routine air emissions are a real concern for immediate neighbors (~1 mile) but not a meaningful health risk at 5 miles.

### 2. Chemical Hazards (Non-Radiological)

- The process uses anhydrous hydrogen fluoride (HF) — one of the most dangerous industrial chemicals
- Retired analytical chemist Michael Ketterer (professor emeritus, Northern Arizona University) calls it "preposterous" to handle ton quantities of uranium, nitric acid, and HF and claim no significant impact
- Radioactive impurities removed during purification include transuranics: plutonium, technetium-99, and neptunium-237 — these end up in solidified liquid process waste shipped to Texas
- The Philadelphia Energy Solutions refinery explosion (2019) involved HF and created a ground-hugging toxic vapor cloud — an example of what catastrophic HF release looks like

### 3. Water — Who Is Exposed and How

**Drinking water sources in the area:**

| Community | Source | Watershed | Exposed to Little Limestone Creek contamination? |
|-----------|--------|-----------|--------------------------------------------------|
| Jonesborough | Nolichucky River (intake near Lamar Community) | Nolichucky | **Yes — directly downstream.** Little Limestone Creek feeds the Nolichucky above the intake. |
| Johnson City | Watauga River (80%) + Unicoi Springs (20%) | Watauga | **No.** Completely separate watershed to the east. |

Jonesborough's regional water system serves a large portion of Washington County — from Unicoi County to the Fall Branch area. Anyone on that system is downstream of the facility. Johnson City draws from the Watauga River and Unicoi Springs, which are in a separate watershed with no hydrological connection to Little Limestone Creek or the Nolichucky above the BWXT site.

**My situation:** I'm on Johnson City water (Watauga River). My drinking water is not in the path of a Little Limestone Creek contamination event. This substantially reduces my personal waterborne exposure risk. However, friends and neighbors on the Jonesborough Regional Water System are directly exposed.

**Other water exposure considerations:**

- Private wells in the area may draw from karst aquifers that connect to Little Limestone Creek's subsurface network (see Karst Geology section below)
- Recreational use of the Nolichucky River (fishing, swimming, rafting) creates another exposure pathway
- Agricultural use — farms irrigating from the Nolichucky or its tributaries

**The floodplain risk is about accidents, not routine discharge.** The facility sits adjacent to Little Limestone Creek. BWXT says all buildings will be outside FEMA flood zones (100-year floodplain). TDEC confirmed the proposed new site contains Waters of the State (streams and wetlands), identified during a joint TDEC/Army Corps site visit on 2/20/2026.

Report 04 (Aaron Knott) makes a compelling case with six U.S. case studies showing how accidents at facilities near waterways escalate into water-supply emergencies:

- Elk River, WV (2014): single containment failure at a chemical facility near a river intake knocked out water for 300,000 people
- Arkema, Crosby TX (2017): Hurricane Harvey flooding disabled safety systems, triggering chemical fires
- Chemtool, Rockton IL (2021): fire at facility adjacent to river — firefighting strategies themselves risked contaminated runoff
- ITC, Deer Park TX (2019): prolonged industrial fire led to multi-million-dollar natural resource damage settlement
- Philadelphia Energy Solutions (2019): HF release from mechanical failure
- East Palestine, OH (2023): hazardous materials incident converted a small waterway into a long-term environmental management issue

The question is not whether BWXT intends to pollute the creek. It's whether the location amplifies consequences when something goes wrong.

### 4. Karst Geology

The area sits on karst limestone — dissolved rock riddled with sinkholes, caves, and underground conduits. Report 06 (Aaron Knott, sourcing USGS and university research) documents:

- Groundwater moves through enlarged cracks and conduits ("underground highways"), not slowly through porous soil — contaminants can travel miles quickly with minimal natural filtration
- 87% of public groundwater in Middle/East Tennessee comes from karst aquifers
- Contaminants can migrate vertically up to 125 meters and horizontally for miles, persisting for decades by getting trapped in rock matrices
- Sinkholes act as natural drains where surface water quickly enters the ground
- The Jonesboro Limestone in this area develops large underground conduits through dissolution
- USGS dye tracing in nearby Morristown confirms karst systems connecting springs and streams over miles

This terrain genuinely amplifies contamination risk. A spill or containment failure that might stay local in normal soil can spread unpredictably through the karst network.

### 5. Historical Precedent

- BWXT acknowledges that **every comparable historical HPDU facility has resulted in EPA Superfund sites** from soil contamination
- Contaminated sites cited: Fernald (OH), Homestake, and a BWXT-owned nuclear fuel refinery in nearby Unicoi County (repeatedly leaked radioactive materials since 1959)
- BWXT's argument: modern practices are fundamentally different — decontamination and decommissioning are included in the current contract scope
- Counter-argument: the same "we're different now" claims were made at every prior facility, and the consequences of being wrong play out over decades

### 6. The "Same Process" Framing

BWXT repeatedly describes this as "the same thing AOT has always done." Their own Q&A documents clarify:

- This means **same end product only** — not same processes, chemicals, operations, or hazard profiles
- Historical operations: metal reduction (50+ year track record at this site)
- New operations: chemical conversion and purification (never performed at production scale at this site in its current configuration)
- New hazards: HF handling, nitric acid dissolution, solvent extraction
- New industrial classification: Basic Inorganic Chemical Manufacturing (NAICS 325180)

BWXT notes the site historically produced UF4 from UF6 through the early 1980s, so chemical operations aren't entirely unprecedented. But the current HPDU process at the planned scale is a new integrated configuration. This is the most misleading element of BWXT's public communication.

### 7. Regulatory Sequencing

BWXT obtained rezoning before completing NEPA review, air dispersion modeling, child-protective risk evaluation, or detailed emissions calculations. Their position: this is standard — rezoning is a threshold land-use decision that precedes technical reviews.

This is **genuinely how the process works** — rezoning before NEPA is normal nationwide. But it means the community approved a land-use change on the basis of BWXT's "design-basis confidence" without the data to verify those claims. The practical effect: now that M-2 zoning is granted, it's essentially irreversible, and the subsequent technical reviews carry less leverage because the fundamental land-use permission has already been given.

## Concrete Risk Scenarios and Preparedness

What could actually go wrong, how would it reach you, and what would you do? These scenarios are ordered roughly from most likely to least likely.

### Scenario A: Chronic Low-Level Contamination of Little Limestone Creek / Nolichucky

**What happens:** Small, ongoing releases — leaks from waste storage, stormwater runoff carrying contaminated sediment, slow seepage through karst — accumulate over years. Uranium and/or processing chemicals (fluoride, nitrates) build up in creek sediment and migrate downstream. This is the pattern at every historical HPDU site that became a Superfund site.

**Who's affected:** People on the Jonesborough Regional Water System, private well users in karst-connected areas, anyone eating fish from or recreating in the Nolichucky downstream. Farmers irrigating from affected tributaries.

**Your exposure (5 mi, Johnson City water):** Low direct exposure. Your drinking water comes from the Watauga watershed. You could be exposed through recreational use of the Nolichucky or locally-sourced food.

**How you'd know:** You probably wouldn't, at first. This is the insidious scenario — detectable only through systematic water and soil monitoring. By the time it shows up in health statistics, decades of exposure have occurred.

**What to do:**

- Support independent baseline water and soil sampling NOW, before full-scale production begins. This is the single highest-value community action. Without a baseline, you can't prove contamination came from BWXT.
- Request TDEC's monthly monitoring reports (Division of Radiological Health performs monthly independent monitoring of air and water). Make this data public and track trends.
- If you have friends on private wells near the site, encourage them to get baseline water testing for uranium, fluoride, and heavy metals.

### Scenario B: HF Release — Equipment Failure or Process Accident

**What happens:** A valve failure, pipe rupture, or process upset releases anhydrous hydrogen fluoride. HF is denser than air and forms a ground-hugging toxic plume that drifts downwind.

**Scale and precedent:**

- **Gumi, South Korea (2012):** 8 tons of HF leaked from a chemical plant. 5 killed, 12,243 people treated for symptoms. Evacuation initially 1.5 km, later expanded. Symptoms reported beyond 1 km included sore throat, headache, cough, eye irritation. Damaged 2.4 km² of agricultural land and 3,200 livestock.
- **Marathon Petroleum, Texas (1987):** 24 tons of HF spilled. 3,000 residents evacuated within 800m within 20 minutes.
- **Philadelphia Energy Solutions (2019):** HF release from mechanical failure created a ground-hugging toxic vapor cloud.
- **Plume modeling research** shows that for large releases, dangerous HF concentrations (>20 ppm) can extend 3+ km downwind. At the extreme end (massive catastrophic release in stable nighttime atmospheric conditions), the 20 ppm contour can reach ~14 km (~8.7 miles).

**Your exposure (5 mi / ~8 km, downwind):** A routine small release wouldn't reach you. A large catastrophic release in worst-case weather conditions (stable atmosphere, low wind, nighttime) could theoretically push irritant-level concentrations to your distance, but this would be an extreme event. Most modeled HF release scenarios show dangerous concentrations dissipating well before 5 miles.

**How you'd know:** Emergency alerts, smell (HF has a sharp, pungent odor detectable at low concentrations), news reports. BWXT is required to have an emergency plan filed with the NRC and coordinated with local emergency services.

**What to do:**

- Know your county's emergency alert system. Make sure you receive Washington County emergency notifications.
- In an HF release: **go indoors, close windows and doors, turn off HVAC systems that draw outside air.** HF is water-soluble and heavier than air — sheltering in an upper floor of a sealed building is protective.
- If you smell a sharp chemical odor or receive an alert: do not go outside to investigate. Shelter in place until all-clear.
- Have a "go bag" and evacuation route planned that takes you upwind (east/north in prevailing conditions).

### Scenario C: Flood Event Overwhelms Containment

**What happens:** A major flood (remember Hurricane Helene hit this area in 2024) inundates the facility grounds. Even if buildings are outside the 100-year floodplain, floodwaters can disable infrastructure, compromise waste storage, and create contaminated runoff pathways to Little Limestone Creek.

**Precedent:** The Arkema Crosby, TX case (Hurricane Harvey 2017) is the closest analogy. Flooding disabled safety systems, causing chemical fires and contamination. East Tennessee is not immune to extreme rainfall — Helene demonstrated this.

**Who's affected:** Downstream water users on the Nolichucky system. Emergency responders. Anyone in the flood-affected corridor.

**Your exposure:** Low direct exposure (you're not in the flood corridor and not on Nolichucky water). Friends on Jonesborough water could face water-supply disruption.

**What to do:**

- After any major flood event in the area, demand immediate public reporting of facility status — was containment maintained? Was any material released? What monitoring was done?
- A flood that disrupts BWXT operations would likely also disrupt water treatment. Have stored water (FEMA recommends 1 gallon/person/day for at least 3 days).

### Scenario D: Fire at the Facility

**What happens:** A fire in processing or storage areas creates smoke containing uranium particulates and/or HF decomposition products. Firefighting water creates contaminated runoff into Little Limestone Creek.

**Precedent:** The Chemtool/Rockton IL case (2021) illustrates the dilemma — aggressive firefighting increases runoff risk to adjacent waterways, while limited suppression prolongs airborne exposure. The ITC Deer Park TX fire (2019) led to multi-million-dollar natural resource damage claims.

**Your exposure:** Smoke plumes from industrial fires can carry particulates for miles. At 5 miles downwind, you could be exposed to smoke and airborne uranium particulates. This is one of the scenarios where your distance is less protective than for routine emissions, because fire lofts material higher and farther than normal stack emissions.

**What to do:**

- Same as HF release: shelter in place, seal the building, turn off HVAC.
- Monitor air quality. A consumer-grade PM2.5 monitor (like a PurpleAir sensor) won't detect uranium specifically but will tell you if particulate levels are elevated.
- If smoke is visible from your location, consider voluntary evacuation upwind until all-clear.

### Scenario E: Transportation Accident

**What happens:** A truck carrying DU feedstock, HF, or process waste is involved in an accident on local roads. BWXT estimates ~4 truck trips per operating day at full scale, ~1.6 of which are hazardous materials.

**Your exposure:** Depends entirely on whether the accident happens on a road near you. The feedstock and waste shipment routes haven't been publicly detailed.

**What to do:**

- During NEPA review, demand disclosure of planned transportation routes for hazardous materials. Know which roads are on those routes.
- Standard hazmat accident guidance applies: stay upwind, stay upstream, stay uphill. Minimum 300m distance from a hazmat spill.

### Scenario F: Long-Term Soil Contamination / Future Superfund

**What happens:** Over the facility's multi-decade lifespan, contamination accumulates in soil and groundwater despite best practices. Decades after operations cease, the site requires remediation. The karst geology means contamination may have spread through underground conduits to locations far from the facility.

**Precedent:** This is the scenario that has played out at every prior comparable facility. BWXT's contract includes decontamination and decommissioning, which is genuinely better than historical practice. But the contract runs through 2035, with potential follow-on contracts extending operations indefinitely. Long-term stewardship commitments from the federal government have a mixed track record.

**Your exposure:** Primarily through property values and community health over very long timeframes. If karst conduits connect the facility to groundwater sources you use (even indirectly), there's a slow-accumulation risk.

**What to do:**

- This is the argument for sustained, long-term independent monitoring — not just during the controversy, but for decades.
- Support any efforts to establish a formal community monitoring program with independent funding (not reliant on BWXT's goodwill).
- Track whether BWXT or DOE establishes a formal site environmental monitoring program with publicly available data.

### Summary: Personal Risk Profile at 5 Miles on Johnson City Water

| Risk | Likelihood | Severity to you personally | Key factor |
|------|-----------|---------------------------|------------|
| Routine air emissions | Ongoing | Very low | Distance and dispersion |
| Chronic water contamination | Moderate over decades | Very low (you're on Watauga water) | Different watershed |
| HF release reaching you | Very low | Low-to-moderate (irritant level at most) | Only in extreme weather + large release |
| Fire smoke plume | Low | Moderate | Fire lofts material farther than stacks |
| Flood overwhelming containment | Low | Very low direct (friends on Nolichucky water are exposed) | You're not on affected water system |
| Transportation accident near you | Very low | Variable | Depends on route |
| Long-term Superfund scenario | Moderate over decades | Low direct, moderate community-wide | Property values, community health |

**Bottom line for you personally:** Your biggest protection is being on Johnson City water (Watauga watershed), which removes the most significant exposure pathway. Your direct health risk from routine operations at 5 miles is very low. The scenarios that could meaningfully affect you are (1) a large fire sending smoke your way, (2) a catastrophic HF release in worst-case atmospheric conditions, or (3) long-term community-wide effects if the site becomes a contamination problem over decades. For friends on Jonesborough water or living closer to the facility, the risk profile is materially different — the water pathway is real and the accident scenarios are more consequential.

## Risk Profile: Friends on Jonesborough Regional Water System

If you're on Jonesborough water, your situation is fundamentally different. Jonesborough draws raw water from the Nolichucky River at an intake near the Lamar Community. Little Limestone Creek — which runs through the BWXT property — feeds into the Nolichucky upstream of that intake. You are directly downstream of the facility.

### What this means for each scenario

**Routine operations** — Jonesborough's water treatment plant uses charcoal filtration, MIOX disinfection, and maintains 5.4 million gallons of storage capacity. Standard water treatment removes most particulates but is not specifically designed to filter dissolved uranium or fluoride compounds. If contamination enters the Nolichucky at low chronic levels, the water plant may or may not catch it depending on the contaminant form and concentration.

**Accident / spill into Little Limestone Creek** — This is your highest-consequence scenario. A containment failure, firefighting runoff, or flood-driven release could send uranium compounds, HF, nitric acid, or process chemicals into the creek and downstream to the Nolichucky, potentially reaching the water intake before the contamination is even identified. In the Elk River, WV case (2014), a single chemical leak upstream of a water intake shut down water service for 300,000 people for days. Early in that incident, authorities didn't know what had been released, in what quantity, or what the health implications were.

**Flood event** — A major flood simultaneously threatens BWXT's containment, mobilizes contaminated sediment, and can overwhelm or disable the water treatment plant. Hurricane Helene (2024) demonstrated that this region is not immune to extreme flooding. A flood that releases material from the BWXT site into Little Limestone Creek while also disrupting Jonesborough's water treatment is a realistic compound scenario.

**Chronic low-level contamination** — The slow-accumulation scenario over years or decades. Uranium, fluoride, or processing chemicals seep into groundwater, migrate through karst conduits to Little Limestone Creek, and enter the Nolichucky at concentrations below what would trigger an emergency but above what's safe for long-term consumption. This is the pattern at historical HPDU sites that became Superfund sites. It's detectable only through systematic monitoring.

### Risk summary for Jonesborough water users

| Risk | Likelihood | Severity | Key factor |
|------|-----------|----------|------------|
| Routine air emissions (if close to facility) | Ongoing | Low-to-moderate within 1 mi; low beyond | Distance |
| Chronic water contamination | Moderate over decades | **Moderate-to-high** | **You are directly downstream** |
| Acute spill reaching water intake | Low for any given year | **High** | Creek → Nolichucky → your tap |
| Flood + containment failure | Low | **High** | Compound event: release + water plant disruption |
| Fire smoke plume | Low | Moderate if close | Distance-dependent |
| Long-term Superfund | Moderate over decades | **High** — property values, health, water supply | Historical precedent at every comparable site |

### What to do

**Immediate — before full-scale production begins:**

1. **Get baseline water quality data.** Request or FOIA the most recent Jonesborough water quality report. Note current levels for uranium, fluoride, gross alpha/beta radiation, and heavy metals. This is your "before" snapshot. If contamination appears later, you need this to prove the change.

2. **Ask Jonesborough's water department directly:** Does the treatment plant test for uranium? How often? What would trigger a shutdown or public advisory? Is there an emergency protocol specifically for upstream chemical releases? These are reasonable questions that ratepayers should be asking.

3. **Sign up for Washington County emergency alerts.** In an acute event, the window between release and contamination reaching the water intake may be short. You need to know before you turn on the tap.

4. **Store water.** FEMA recommends 1 gallon per person per day for at least 3 days. Given the specific risk of upstream contamination, having a week's supply is reasonable. This is inexpensive insurance.

5. **Consider a home water filter rated for uranium and fluoride removal.** Reverse osmosis systems remove both. This is not necessary today — current water quality is presumably fine — but worth considering as a long-term hedge once production scales up. Costs $200-500 for an under-sink RO system.

**Ongoing — once production is operational:**

1. **Track TDEC monitoring data.** TDEC's Division of Radiological Health performs monthly independent monitoring of air and water for radioactive effluents at the facility. Request this data regularly. It's public information.

2. **Support independent water monitoring.** Community-funded independent sampling of Little Limestone Creek and the Nolichucky upstream and downstream of the facility, conducted quarterly or after significant weather events. This creates accountability that doesn't depend on BWXT or TDEC.

3. **Engage in the NEPA process.** When DOE/NNSA conducts the required environmental review for the full-scale facility, submit comments demanding:
   - Detailed spill-scenario modeling for Little Limestone Creek → Nolichucky → water intake
   - Specific emergency notification protocols for downstream water systems
   - Continuous (not just periodic) water monitoring at the creek-to-river confluence
   - A formal agreement between BWXT and Jonesborough's water system for emergency communication

4. **Watch the NPDES water permit renewal.** The existing discharge permit (TN0057983) is currently being renewed. The new HPDU chemical processes will change the waste stream. This is a public comment opportunity — use it.

5. **Know your fallback water options.** If Jonesborough water is shut down due to upstream contamination, where do you get water? Johnson City's system (Watauga watershed) is independent. Bottled water. Mutual aid from neighboring systems. Think about this before you need it.

## Risk Profile: Friends on Private Wells

If you're on a private well in the area around the BWXT facility, your risk profile is different from both municipal water users — and in some ways more concerning, because you have less institutional protection.

### Why private wells are uniquely vulnerable here

**No treatment plant between you and contamination.** Municipal water systems have filtration, treatment, and testing protocols. A private well delivers groundwater directly to your tap. If contaminants reach your aquifer, they reach you.

**Karst geology creates unpredictable connections.** Report 06 documents that this area sits on karst limestone where groundwater moves through underground conduits — not slowly through porous soil, but rapidly through dissolved channels that can connect surface water to aquifers over miles. A contaminant entering a sinkhole or seeping into a fracture near the BWXT site could emerge in a well miles away, and the path it takes is largely unmapped and unpredictable. USGS dye tracing in nearby Morristown has confirmed these kinds of long-distance karst connections.

**No regulatory requirement to test your well for uranium.** EPA's maximum contaminant level (MCL) for uranium in drinking water is 30 µg/L — but that standard only applies to public water systems. No one is required to test your private well. If contamination reaches your aquifer, you won't know unless you test for it yourself.

**You may already have naturally elevated uranium.** East Tennessee's karst limestone can contain naturally occurring uranium. Some private wells in the region may already have measurable uranium levels from natural geology, independent of BWXT. This makes baseline testing doubly important — you need to know what's natural before you can detect what's industrial.

### Risk summary for private well users

| Risk | Likelihood | Severity | Key factor |
|------|-----------|----------|------------|
| Routine air emissions | Ongoing | Low (unless very close) | Distance-dependent |
| Chronic groundwater contamination | **Moderate-to-high over decades** | **High** | Karst conduits, no treatment, no required monitoring |
| Acute spill reaching aquifer | Low for any given year | **High** | Karst transport can be fast and unpredictable |
| Flood mobilizing contaminants into karst | Low-to-moderate | **High** | Sinkholes act as drains during floods |
| Long-term Superfund / property impact | Moderate over decades | **Very high** | Well contamination can render property difficult to sell |

### What to do

**Immediate — this is urgent and should not wait:**

1. **Get your well water tested NOW — before full-scale production begins.** This is the single most important thing a private well owner near this site can do. Test for:
   - **Uranium** (EPA MCL: 30 µg/L) — ~$35 for a basic uranium test through a certified lab
   - **Fluoride** (EPA MCL: 4 mg/L; secondary standard: 2 mg/L)
   - **Gross alpha and gross beta radiation** — screens for a range of radionuclides
   - **Nitrate** (a byproduct of the nitric acid dissolution process)
   - **Heavy metals panel** (including nickel, chromium, lead)
   - **pH and conductivity** — baseline indicators that can flag changes over time

   Where to get tested in Tennessee:
   - TDEC maintains a list of [Commercial Laboratories Approved for Drinking Water Analysis](https://www.tn.gov/environment/program-areas/wr-water-resources/water-quality/drinking-water-program0.html)
   - Contact TDEC's drinking water program at 1-888-891-TDEC (8332) for guidance
   - National mail-in labs like [Tap Score](https://mytapscore.com/) or [National Testing Laboratories](https://watercheck.com/) offer comprehensive well water panels ($90-200)
   - Basic uranium-only testing is available for ~$35 through labs like [ETR Labs](https://etrlabs.com/product/uranium/)

2. **Document your baseline.** Save the lab results with the date. This is your proof of pre-production water quality. If contamination appears in future testing, you need this to establish that it wasn't there before.

3. **Identify your well's geology.** If possible, find out what formation your well draws from. Wells in limestone or dolomite formations (Knox Group, Chickamauga Group, Jonesboro Limestone) are in karst rock and are more vulnerable to rapid contaminant transport. Your well driller's log (if available) or a county geological survey may help.

4. **Check for sinkholes on or near your property.** Sinkholes are direct entry points for surface contamination into the karst groundwater system. If you have sinkholes nearby, your well is more connected to surface events at the BWXT site.

**Ongoing — once production is operational:**

1. **Test your well water annually.** The same panel as your baseline. Track changes over time. This costs $90-200/year — a modest price for knowing what you're drinking.

2. **Test after major weather events.** Heavy rains and floods flush contaminants through karst systems rapidly. If there's a major storm or flood in the area, test your water within a few weeks.

3. **Consider a reverse osmosis (RO) system.** An under-sink RO system ($200-500) removes uranium, fluoride, and most dissolved contaminants. It's not necessary if your current levels are safe, but it's a meaningful layer of protection if you're in a karst-connected area near the facility. RO systems need filter replacement (~$50-100/year).

4. **Connect with neighbors who are also on wells.** Multiple wells being tested creates a monitoring network. If contamination shows up in one well, neighboring wells should be tested immediately. A community-organized well-testing program is far more powerful than individual action.

5. **Engage in the NEPA process.** When DOE/NNSA conducts the environmental review, demand:
   - Karst hydrogeological characterization of the site and surrounding area — where do the subsurface conduits go?
   - Groundwater monitoring wells between the facility and the nearest private wells
   - A private well monitoring program funded by BWXT or DOE for wells within a defined radius

6. **Know when to stop drinking the water.** If any test shows uranium above 30 µg/L, fluoride above 4 mg/L, or gross alpha above 15 pCi/L, switch to bottled or filtered water immediately and contact TDEC. These are EPA MCLs — exceeding them means the water is not safe for long-term consumption.

### The hard truth about private wells and karst

Municipal water users have institutional protection — a treatment plant, required testing, public accountability. Private well owners have none of that. You are your own water utility. In karst terrain next to a chemical facility that will handle thousands of tons of uranium and hydrogen fluoride over decades, being your own water utility means being proactive about testing, because by the time contamination is obvious (taste, odor, color), exposure has likely been occurring for a long time.

The karst geology is not speculative risk — it's documented by USGS, confirmed by dye tracing studies in this region, and is the reason 87% of public groundwater in Middle/East Tennessee comes from these formations. Contaminants that enter the karst system can travel miles through underground conduits with minimal filtration and emerge in wells, springs, and streams far from the source.

## Evaluating Argument Quality — Both Sides

### Where BWXT's arguments are strong

- **Regulatory sequencing is standard.** Rezoning before NEPA is normal procedure, not a suspicious maneuver.
- **The "insignificant activity" determination is real.** TDEC — not BWXT — made that classification for the pilot plant.
- **Depleted uranium is genuinely low-radioactivity material.** 40% less radioactive than natural uranium is factually correct. The radiological risk from DU is primarily from inhalation of fine particles at close range, not from living miles away.
- **The rezoned-vs-existing-M2 siting comparison is legitimate.** Greater buffer distances, better traffic flow, less visibility are real site-planning benefits.

### Where BWXT's arguments are weak or evasive

- **"Same thing AOT has always done"** — misleading by their own admission (see above).
- **Deferring all quantitative answers to "future permitting" while simultaneously claiming confidence.** You can't have "design-basis confidence" and also not have finished the design. The Feb 2026 Follow-Up Q&A (question 9) directly asks about this contradiction, and BWXT's answer is essentially "trust the process."
- **Child cancer risk dismissal.** Calling concern for children a "rhetorical tactic, not a scientific conclusion" (Follow-Up Q&A question 6) is tone-deaf and scientifically lazy. Children have higher intake-to-body-weight ratios, faster cell division, and longer remaining lifespans for latent effects. Pediatric risk factors are standard EPA risk assessment methodology, not rhetoric.
- **"Binding community commitments are inappropriate."** When asked what concrete commitments they'd make, BWXT said none — because binding commitments would create "improper conditional zoning." Legally defensible, practically useless.
- **Historical Superfund sites.** "Modern practices are different" is the right answer, but BWXT undercuts it by refusing to provide the specific data that would demonstrate how different.

### Where community opposition arguments are strong

- **Karst geology + floodplain + drinking water watershed.** Report 06 is well-sourced, the USGS data is real, and the terrain genuinely amplifies contamination risk.
- **Accident-scenario framing.** Report 04's argument that floodplain risk is about accidents, not routine discharge, is the strongest analytical contribution. The case studies are relevant.
- **The Superfund track record.** Every prior comparable facility. Not some. Every one.
- **Spot zoning analysis.** Report 02 makes a technically competent legal argument. The planning commission agreed.

### Where community opposition arguments are weaker

- **Conflating bounding-case permit numbers with expected emissions.** The 250 lbs/year uranium dust figure from the permit filing is an intentionally conservative worst case. Treating it as the expected emission level is cherry-picking in the other direction.
- **Treating DU as if it's enriched uranium or spent fuel.** Some community rhetoric implies a level of radiological hazard that doesn't match DU's actual properties. DU is a heavy metal toxin and alpha emitter, but calibrating the fear correctly matters for credibility.
- **Aaron Knott's reports are informed advocacy, not neutral expertise.** He discloses this clearly in his disclaimers, and the reports are well-researched, but they are opinion and public commentary. The legal arguments (spot zoning, comprehensive plan) are strongest; environmental/health claims are sometimes overstated.

## What Levers Remain

### 1. NEPA Environmental Review (biggest lever)

BWXT/NNSA/DOE must conduct a new NEPA review for the full-scale facility. TDEC confirmed the existing EA (DOE/EA-2252 from Nov 2024) did not cover the BWXT HPDU award. This is where:

- Air dispersion modeling will actually be done
- Receptor-distance analysis (schools, residences) will be completed
- Child-protective risk evaluation should be demanded
- There will be a public comment period

**Push for a full Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)** rather than a lighter Environmental Assessment (EA). An EIS is more rigorous, requires more public participation, and considers alternatives. The case for an EIS is strong: $1.6B sole-source national facility, novel production-scale process, karst/floodplain setting.

### 2. Air Permitting for Full-Scale Plant

The "insignificant activity" determination covers only the pilot plant (~20 MT/year). The full-scale facility (300-400 MT/year) will need its own air quality review. Watch whether BWXT files this as another "insignificant activity" or whether TDEC requires a full air permit.

### 3. NPDES Water Permit Renewal

The existing water discharge permit (TN0057983) is being renewed. The new chemical manufacturing steps change the waste stream. Public comment opportunity.

### 4. Aquatic Resource Alteration Permit (ARAP)

The proposed site contains Waters of the State. If construction impacts streams or wetlands, an ARAP is required — another public process.

### 5. Section 106 Historic Preservation Review

Requires consultation with Cherokee Nation and Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians. Separate track that can raise additional issues.

### 6. Independent Monitoring

- **Fund independent soil and water sampling _before full-scale production begins._** Establishing baseline measurements is critical — once production starts, you can't distinguish new contamination from old without a baseline.
- **Request TDEC's monthly monitoring data** — TDEC performs monthly independent monitoring of air and water for radioactive effluents. This data should be publicly requestable.
- Michael Ketterer has already done soil sampling near BWXT's Telford site and downstream of Nuclear Fuel Services in Erwin — a potential resource.

### 7. Legal Challenge

The spot-zoning arguments in Report 02 provide a potential basis for challenging the rezoning, though this is expensive and uncertain. The 10-5 vote suggests limited political appetite for reversal.
